Review of Measure Theory

analysis
Author

Shyam Ravishankar

Published

The first topic I plan to cover in my blog is some abstract harmonic analysis, including existence and uniqueness of Haar measure, the proof of Pontryagin duality, and Fourier inversion. In preparation for this, I will begin with a review of the fundamental concepts of measure theory. Most theorems will be stated without proof, but I will attempt to give helpful intuition or examples where possible. I assume the reader is familiar with standard real and complex analysis (i.e. at an undergraduate level), although it may be difficult to learn measure theory for the first time from the following summary. Some familiarity with functional analysis may also be helpful for some of the measure theory. Finally, I will also assume the reader is familiar with basic point-set topology and group theory.

For more details on the following material, see any of the following: (Halmos 1974), (Cohn 2013), (Rudin 1987).

\(\sigma\)-algebras and measure spaces

Let \(X\) be a set; we write \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) for the power set of \(X\). A \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(X\) is a set \(\Sigma \subset \mathcal{P}(X)\) satisfying the following axioms:

  1. \(X \in \Sigma\);
  2. \(\Sigma\) is closed under complements: If \(A \in \Sigma\) then \(A^c = X\setminus A \in \Sigma\);
  3. \(\Sigma\) is closed under countable unions: If \(\set{A_i \colon i\in \mathbf{N}} \subset \Sigma\) then \(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i \in \Sigma\).

It follows from 1. and 2. that \(\varnothing \in \Sigma\) and it follows from 2. and 3. that \(\Sigma\) is closed under countable intersections. The sets \(E \in \Sigma\) are called the measurable sets of \(X\). A pair \((X,\Sigma)\) is called a measurable space; for convenience we will usually just denote a measurable space by \(X\), omitting the \(\sigma\)-algebra.

A (positive) measure on \(X\) is a function \(\mu \colon \Sigma \to [0,\infty]\) such that

\[\mu\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mu(A_i)\]

for any countable collection \(\set{A_i \colon i\in \mathbf{N}} \subset \Sigma\) of pairwise disjoint measurable sets. Note that we allow \(\mu\) to take on the value \(\infty\), and the previous equality must still hold true even when one (and thus both) side is infinite. We say that \(\mu\) is countably additive. Two immediate consequences of this definition are that \(\mu(B) \leq \mu(A)\) whenever \(A,B \in \Sigma\) and \(B\subset A\), and

\[ \mu \left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mu(A_i)\]

for any countable collection \(\set{A_i \colon i\in \mathbf{N}} \subset \Sigma\) of measurable sets. The triple \((X, \Sigma, \mu)\) is called a measure space. As before, we will often write \(X\) for a measure space, omitting the \(\sigma\)-algebra and the measure.

When \(\mu(A) = 0\), we call \(A\) a null set. We say a measure \(\mu\) is complete if whenever \(A\) is a null set, we have \(\mathcal{P}(A) \subset \Sigma\). We say \(\mu\) is finite if \(\mu(X) < \infty\) and we say \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite if \(X\) can be written as the union of a countable collection of finite-measure sets.

Examples of \(\sigma\)-algebras and measures

The sets \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) and \(\set{\varnothing, X}\) are simple examples of \(\sigma\)-algebras. A less trivial but still important example is the Borel algebra \(\mathcal{B}(X)\). When \(X\) is a topological space, we define \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) to be the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing the open sets of \(X\). This \(\sigma\)-algebra is important as it will allow us to frequently tie together such notions as measurability and continuity. Another important \(\sigma\)-algebra in real analysis is the Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra. These are defined to be the sets \(E \subset \mathbf{R}\) such that

\[ \mu^*(A) = \mu^*(A \cap E) + \mu^*(A \cap E^c) \quad \text{for all } A \subset \mathbf{R}\]

(here \(\mu^*\) denotes Lebesgue outer measure).

The simplest example of a measure is the counting measure defined on \(\Sigma = \mathcal{P}(X)\) by \(\mu(A) = |A|\) is the cardinality of \(A\). This is the natural measure on countable sets such as \(\mathbf{N}\) and \(\mathbf{Z}\), and we notice that in such cases counting measure is \(\sigma\)-finite. Another common example of a measure is Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbf{R}^n\) (where \(\Sigma\) is the aforementioned Lebesgue \(\sigma\)-algebra); this is an example of a complete \(\sigma\)-finite Borel measure1. Finally, the object of the next few blog posts will be Haar measure on locally compact abelian groups, an example of which we have already in Lebesgue measure.

Outer Measures and Carathéodory’s criterion

A common approach to construct Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbf{R}^n\) is via the Lebesgue outer measure and Carathéodory’s criterion. Another approach is to use the Riesz Representation Theorem (to be discussed later in this post). A third, less common approach would be to define Lebesgue measure as the completion of the normalized Haar measure on \(\mathbf{R}^n\). An outer measure is essentially a weaker version of a measure that is defined on all of \(\mathcal{P}(X)\). More precisely, a function \(\mu^* \colon \mathcal{P}(X) \to [0,\infty]\) is called an outer measure if the following hold:

  1. Empty set is null: \(\mu^*(\varnothing) = 0\);
  2. Monotone: If \(A \subset B\) then \(\mu^*(A) \leq \mu^*(B)\);
  3. Countably subadditive: If \(\{A_i \colon i\in \mathbf{N}\} \subset \mathcal{P}(X)\), then

\[ \mu^*\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty A_i \right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty \mu^*(A_i). \]

The Lebesgue outer measure on \(\mathbf{R}^n\) is defined as

\[ \mu^*(E) \coloneqq \inf\left\{ \sum_{i=1}^\infty V(B_i) \colon E \subset \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty B_i \right\} \]

where \(B_i\) are balls in \(\mathbf{R}^n\) and \(V(B_i)\) denotes the \(n\)-volume of \(B_i\). The Lebesgue measurable sets are defined to be those sets \(E \subset \mathbf{R}^n\) which satisfy

\[ \mu^*(A) = \mu^*(A \cap E) + \mu^*(A \cap E^c) \]

for all \(A \subset \mathbf{R}^n\). One can then verify that these sets form a \(\sigma\)-algebra and that the Borel sets are Lebesgue measurable. This extends more generally to any outer measure in the following form:

Theorem 1 (Carathéodory’s criterion) Let \(\mu^*\) be an outer measure on \(X\); then the sets \(E \subset X\) which satisfy

\[ \mu^*(A) = \mu^*(A \cap E) + \mu^*(A \cap E^c) \]

form a \(\sigma\)-algebra, and \(\mu^*\) forms a measure when restricted to this \(\sigma\)-algebra.

This is a powerful tool for constructing measures; in particular (as in the construction of Haar measure we will see in a future post), to construct a Borel measure on \(X\), it suffices to construct an outer measure on \(X\) and show that the open sets satisfy Carathéodory’s criterion.

Measurable functions and integration

Let \(X\) be a measure space and \(Y\) a topological space. We say \(f \colon X \to Y\) is measurable if \(f^{-1}(E)\) is measurable for all open \(E\subset Y\). In the case where \(Y\) is the extended real number line \(\overline{\mathbf{R}} = [-\infty, \infty]\) it suffices to check that \(f^{-1}((\alpha, \infty])\) is measurable for all \(\alpha \in \mathbf{R}\). An important class of measurable functions \(X \to \overline{\mathbf{R}}\) (resp. to \(\mathbf{R}\) or \(\mathbf{C}\)) are the indicator or characteristic functions for measurable sets \(E\subset X\):

\[ \mathbf{1}_E(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x \in E\\ 0 & \text{if } x \notin E \end{cases}.\]

A complex-valued function on \(X\) is called simple if it has finite range. We note that any measurable simple function \(s\) can be written as

\[ s = \sum_{k=1}^n \alpha_k \mathbf{1}_{E_k} \]

for some \(\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_n \in \mathbf{C}\) and measurable sets \(E_1,\ldots, E_n\). The following theorem is a fundamental result that will allow us to develop the theory of integration on arbitrary measure spaces.

Theorem 2 (Approximation by simple functions) Let \(f \colon X \to [0,\infty]\) be measurable. There exist measurable simple functions \(s_n \colon X \to [0,\infty]\) such that

  1. \(0 \leq s_1 \leq s_2 \leq \cdots \leq f\) (these inequalities hold when each function is evaluated at the same \(x\), for any \(x \in X\)).
  2. \(s_n(x) \to f(x)\) as \(n \to \infty\) for all \(x \in X\).

Let \(s = \sum_{k=1}^n \alpha_k\mathbf{1}_{E_k}\) be a measurable simple function with codomain \([0,\infty]\). For any measurable \(A \subset X\) we define

\[ \int_A s \, d\mu \coloneqq \sum_{k=1}^n \alpha_k \mu(A \cap E_k). \]

For any measurable \(f \colon X \to [0,\infty]\) we define

\[ \int_A f \, d\mu \coloneqq \sup \left\{\int_A s \, d\mu \colon 0 \leq s \leq f, s \text{ measurable simple} \right\}. \]

More generally for \(f \colon X \to \overline{\mathbf{R}}\), we write \(f = f_+ - f_-\) where \(f_+ \coloneqq \max\{ f, 0\}\) and \(f_- \coloneqq -\min\{ f, 0\}\) are the positive and negative parts of \(f\). Since \(f_+,f_-\) map to \([0,\infty]\) we can define

\[ \int_A f \, d\mu \coloneqq \int_A f_+ \, d\mu - \int_A f_- \, d\mu. \]

Finally, if \(f \colon X \to \mathbf{C}\), we write \(f = g + ih\) where \(g,h\) map to \(\mathbf{R}\) and define

\[ \int_A f \, d\mu \coloneqq \int_A g \, d\mu + i\int_A h \, d\mu. \]

We note that if \(f \colon X \to [0,\infty]\) is measurable, then the function \(\nu \colon X \to [0,\infty]\) defined by

\[ \nu(A) \coloneqq \int_A f \, d\mu \]

is a measure on \((X,\Sigma)\), and we have

\[ \int_X g \, d\nu = \int_X gf \, d\mu \]

for all measurable \(g \colon X \to [0,\infty]\).

Properties of measurable functions and integration

Assume \(f,g\) are measurable, \(\alpha \in \mathbf{C}\), and \(A,B\in \Sigma\). Then the following hold:

  1. \(f+g\), \(\alpha f\), and \(|f|\) are measurable;
  2. If \(f\leq g\) then \(\int_A f \, d\mu \leq \int_A g \, d\mu\);
  3. If \(A \subset B\) and \(f \geq 0\) then \(\int_A f \, d\mu \leq \int_B f \, d\mu\);
  4. \(\int_A f+g \, d\mu = \int_A f \, d\mu + \int_A g \, d\mu\) and \(\int_A \alpha f \, d\mu = \alpha \int_A f \, d\mu\);
  5. If \(f \equiv 0\) then \(\int_A f \, d\mu = 0\);
  6. If \(\mu(A) = 0\) then \(\int_A f \, d\mu = 0\);
  7. \(\int_A f \, d\mu = \int_X \mathbf{1}_A f \, d\mu\);
  8. If \(h \colon \mathbf{C}\to \mathbf{C}\) is measurable then \(h\circ f\) is measurable.

Suppose now that \((f_n)\) is a sequence of measurable functions on \(X\); the following also hold:

  1. If each \(f_n\) maps to \(\overline{\mathbf{R}}\) then \(\sup_n f_n(x)\) and \(\inf_n f_n(x)\) are measurable;
  2. If each \(f_n\) maps to \(\overline{\mathbf{R}}\) then \(\limsup_n f_n(x)\) and \(\liminf_n f_n(x)\) are measurable;
  3. If \(f(x) \coloneqq \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x)\) exists for each \(x\in X\) then \(f\) is measurable.

We state now some important convergence theorems for integration; in my opinion it is these theorems that give the theory of Lebesgue integration its power.

Theorem 3 (Monotone Convergence Theorem) Let \((f_n)\) be a sequence of (extended) real-valued measurable functions on \(X\) such that

  1. \(0 \leq f_1 \leq f_2 \leq \cdots \leq \infty\);
  2. \(f_n(x) \to f(x)\) for all \(x\in X\).

Then \(f\) is measurable and

\[ \int_X f_n \, d\mu \to \int_X f\, d\mu. \]

Corollary 1 Let \((f_n)\) be a sequence of nonnegative measurable functions on \(X\). Then

\[ \sum_{n=1}^\infty \int_X f_n \, d\mu = \int_X \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n \, d\mu. \]

Lemma 1 (Fatou’s Lemma) Suppose \((f_n)\) is a sequence of nonnegative measurable functions on \(X\). Then

\[ \int_X \liminf_n f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int_X f_n \, d\mu. \]

Corollary 2 (Fatou-Lebesgue Theorem) Let \((f_n)\) be a sequence of real-valued measurable functions, and suppose there exists a measurable function \(g\) with the property that \(\int_X g \, d\mu < \infty\) (we say \(g\) is integrable). If \(|f_n(x)| \leq g(x)\) for all \(n\) and all \(x\in X\), then all \(f_n\) as well as \(\limsup_n f_n\) and \(\liminf_n f_n\) are integrable, and

\[ \int_X \liminf_n f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int_X f_n \, d\mu \leq \limsup_n \int_X f_n \, d\mu \leq \int_X \limsup_n f_n \, d\mu. \]

Theorem 4 (Dominated Convergence Theorem) Let \((f_n)\) be a sequence of measurable functions on \(X\) such that \(f(x) \coloneqq \lim_{n\to\infty} f_n(x)\) exists for all \(x\in X\). If there is an integrable function \(g\) such that \(|f_n(x)| \leq g(x)\) for all \(n\) and all \(x\in X\), then \(f\) is integrable and

\[ \lim_{n\to\infty} \int_X f_n \, d\mu = \int_X f \, d\mu. \]

We note that, as is common in the theory of integration, theorem conditions stated to hold for all \(x \in X\) can generally be weakened to hold \(\mu\)-almost everywhere i.e. for all \(x \in X\setminus E\) where \(E\) is some measurable set with \(\mu(E) = 0\).

A Flavor of Functional Analysis

The results in this section have a functional analysis-type flavor to them. Nevertheless, they are still what I would consider to be core measure-theoretic results.

Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space and \(\mathcal{B}\) its Borel \(\sigma\)-algebra. A Radon measure on \(X\) is a nonnegative Borel measure which satisfies the following:

  1. Compact Finiteness: \(\mu(K) < \infty\) for all compact \(K\subset X\).
  2. Inner Regularity: \(\mu(U) = \sup\left\{\mu(K) \colon K\subset U, \, K \text{ compact} \right\}\) for all open \(U \subset X\).
  3. Outer Regularity: \(\mu(A) = \inf\left\{\mu(U) \colon S \subset U, \, U \text{ open} \right\}\) for all \(A \in \mathcal{B}\).

We let \(C_c(X)\) denote the continuous complex-valued functions on \(X\) with compact support. If \(\mu\) is a Radon measure on \(X\) then

\[ f \mapsto \int_X f \, d\mu \]

is a positive linear functional functional on \(C_c(X)\)2. The following theorem3 provides a converse to this statement:

Theorem 5 (Riesz Representation Theorem) Let \(\Lambda\) be a positive linear functional on \(C_c(X)\). There exists a unique complete Radon measure on \(X\) such that \(\Lambda f = \int_X f \, d\mu\) for all \(f\in C_c(X)\). Further, this measure is outer regular on all \(\mu\)-measurable sets and inner regular on all finite-measure sets.

Proof. I will not prove this theorem in full; rather I will describe the key step of constructing \(\mu\). For each open \(U \subset X\) we define

\[ \mu(U) \coloneqq \sup\left\{ \Lambda f \colon f \in C_c(X), \, 0 \leq f \leq 1, \, \mathrm{supp} f \subset U \right\}. \]

Then for \(E \subset X\) we set

\[ \mu(E) \coloneqq \inf \left\{ \mu(U) \colon E \subset U, \, U \text{ open} \right\}; \]

although we have defined \(\mu(E)\) for every subset of \(X\), much of the proof of the Riesz Representation Theorem involves restricting \(E\) to be in the “right” \(\sigma\)-algebra so that \(\mu\) is a measure with the desired properties.

\(\square\)

In light of the above theorem, it is sometimes practice to call any positive linear functional on \(C_c(X)\) a Radon integral on \(X\). As mentioned earlier, we can use this theorem to construct Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbf{R}^n\) by defining the functional \(\Lambda\) to map \(f \in C_c(\mathbf{R}^n)\) to \(\int_{\mathbf{R}^n} f(x) \, dx\), its Riemann integral over \(\mathbf{R}^n\).

We now let \(X\) be an arbitrary measure space, and we define for \(1 \leq p <\infty\) the \(L^p\) spaces

\[ L^p(\mu) \coloneqq \left\{f \colon \int_X |f|^p \, d\mu < \infty \right\} \]

where each \(f\) is understood to be measurable in order for the integral to make sense. For \(p = \infty\) we define

\[ L^\infty(\mu) \coloneqq \left\{ f \colon f \text{ measurable}, \, \operatorname{esssup}|f| < \infty \right\} \]

where \(\operatorname{esssup}\) is the essential supremum4

\[ \operatorname{esssup}g \coloneqq \inf\{\alpha \in \overline{\mathbf{R}} \colon \mu\left( g^{-1}(\alpha, \infty]\right) = 0\}. \]

On the \(L^p\) spaces we define the \(p\)-norm \(\|\cdot\|_p\) by

\[ \| f \|_p \coloneqq \begin{cases} \left( \int_X |f|^p \, d\mu \right)^{1/p} & 1\leq p <\infty\\ \operatorname{esssup}|f| & p = \infty \end{cases}.\]

Since the \(p\)-norm is blind to null sets, we consider the \(L^p\) spaces to be equivalence classes of functions, with equivalence given by

\[ (f \equiv g) \iff (f = g \quad \mu-\text{almost everywhere}) \iff (\| f-g \|_p = 0). \]

The \(L^p\) spaces are then Banach spaces under the \(p\)-norm i.e. they are complete metric spaces with the metric \(d(f,g) = \|f-g\|_p\). When \(X\) is a locally compact Hausdorff space and \(\mu\) a complete Radon measure, we have that \(C_c(X)\) is dense in \(L^p(\mu)\) for all \(1\leq p < \infty\).

Product Measures and Fubini’s theorem

Let \((X,\Sigma,\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{T},\nu)\) be measure spaces; we define a measure on the cartesian product \(X\times Y\) as follows. We define \(\Sigma \times \mathcal{T}\) to be the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra on \(X \times Y\) which contains all sets of the form \(A\times B\) where \(A\in \Sigma\) and \(B \in \mathcal{T}\). The product measure \(\mu \times \nu\) is then defined to be a measure on \((X\times Y, \Sigma \times \mathcal{T})\) such that \((\mu \times \nu)(A \times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B)\) for all \(A \in \Sigma\), \(B \in \mathcal{T}\). We note that if \(X\) and \(Y\) are both topological spaces, and \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are both Borel measures, then \(\mu \times \nu\) is a Borel measure on \(X\times Y\) endowed with the product topology. If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are both \(\sigma\)-finite, then the product measure \(\mu \times \nu\) is unique and is also \(\sigma\)-finite5.

Suppose now that \(X,Y\) are \(\sigma\)-finite, let \(E \in \Sigma \times \mathcal{T}\), and for each \((x,y) \in X\times Y\), define the sets

\[ E_x \coloneqq \{y \in Y \colon (x,y) \in E\} \qquad \text{and} \qquad E^y \coloneqq \{x \in X \colon (x,y) \in E\}. \]

Then \(E_x \in \mathcal{T}\) and \(E^y \in \Sigma\) and the functions \(x\mapsto \nu(E_x)\) and \(y \mapsto \mu(E^y)\) are \(\Sigma\)- and \(\mathcal{T}\)-measurable, respectively. We further have the equality

\[ (\mu \times \nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x) \, d\mu(x) = \int_Y \mu(E^y) \, d\nu(y). \]

For a \((\Sigma \times \mathcal{T})\)-measurable function \(f\), we similarly define \(f_x = f(x,y)\) and \(f^y = f(x,y)\) for fixed \((x,y) \in X \times Y\). Then \(f_x\) is \(\mathcal{T}\)-measurable and \(f^y\) is \(\Sigma\)-measurable.

With this we can now state Fubini’s Theorem6:

Theorem 6 (Fubini’s Theorem) Let \((X,\Sigma,\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{T},\nu)\) be locally compact Hausdorff measure spaces. For \(f \in C_c(X\times Y)\), the functions

\[ x \mapsto \int_Y f_x \, d\nu \qquad \text{and} \qquad y \mapsto \int_X f^y \, d\mu \]

are in \(C_c(X)\) and \(C_c(Y)\), respectively. Further, we have the equality

\[ \int_{X \times Y} f \, d(\mu \times \nu) = \int_X \left(\int_Y f_x \, d\nu \right) \, d\mu = \int_Y \left(\int_X f^y \, d\mu \right) \, d\nu. \]
