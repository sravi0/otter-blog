This first post is mostly a humorous argument1 that prime numbers are not square, though there are some connections to important concepts in algebraic number theory which I will highlight at the end.

“Proof” that primes are not square

Let \(l\) be an arbitrary prime number. In order to show \(l\) is not a square integer, our goal will be to construct a number field \(K\) in which squares are local norms everywhere i.e. for every prime \(\mathfrak{p}\) of \(K\), the square integers are in the norm group \(\mathrm{Norm}_{K_{\mathfrak{p}}/\mathbb{Q}_p}(K_{\mathfrak{p}}^\times)\). If we can construct such a \(K\) with the additional property that \(l\) is not a local norm at some place2, then we know that \(l\) cannot be a square. To start off, we can use the Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT) and Dirichlet’s Theorem on primes in arithmetic progressions to choose a prime \(p\) which is congruent to \(1 \bmod 4\) such that \(p \not\equiv\square \bmod l\) (\(p\) is not a square modulo \(l\)). Similarly, we can use CRT and Dirichlet’s theorem to choose a prime \(q\) which is different from \(p\), satisfying \(q \equiv 1 \bmod 4\) and \(q \equiv \square \bmod p\).

Our choice of \(p\) and \(q\) enforces that \(K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p},\sqrt{q})\) satifies the following:

  • As \(p,q \equiv 1 \bmod 4\), the only primes ramifying in \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p})\) and \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q})\) are \(p\) and \(q\), respectively. Thus \(p\) and \(q\) are the only primes which ramify in \(K\).
  • Since \(q \equiv \square \bmod p\), we know that \(p\) splits in \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q})\). Since each prime is \(1 \bmod 4\) the law of quadratic reciprocity implies that \(p \equiv \square \bmod q\). So \(q\) also splits in \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p})\).
  • No prime can be completely inert in \(K\), as \(C_2 \times C_2\) cannot surject onto \(C_4\). Thus every local extension \(K_{\mathfrak{r}}/\mathbb{Q}_r\) has degree \(1\) or \(2\) as the ramified primes (\(p\) and \(q\)) have degree \(2\) local extensions.
  • All rational squares are local norms everywhere: if \(K_{\mathfrak{r}}/\mathbb{Q}_r\) has degree \(1\) then \(x^2\) is the norm of \(x^2\). Otherwise \(K_{\mathfrak{r}}/\mathbb{Q}_r\) is degree \(2\), in which case \(x^2\) is the norm of \(x\).
  • Since \(p \not\equiv\square \bmod l\) and \(p \equiv 1 \bmod 4\), the law of quadratic reciprocity gives that \(l \not\equiv\square \bmod p\). This means \(l\) is inert in \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p})\) and thus in \(K\) as well. Per the previous points, the extension \(K_{\mathfrak{l}}/\mathbb{Q}_l\) must have degree \(2\) so the norm image is \(l^{2\mathbb{Z}} \times \mathbb{Z}_l^\times\). This means \(l\) is not a local norm at itself.

As desired, we have constructed an extension \(K/\mathbb{Q}\) in which all squares are local norms everywhere but \(l\) is not a local norm at itself. Thus \(l\) cannot be a square, and since \(l\) was arbitrary we have shown that no prime is a square!

Serious Discussion: the Hasse Norm Theorem

The inspiration for this argument came from a construction in algebraic number theory relating to the following theorem:

Theorem 1 (Hasse Norm Theorem) Let \(L/K\) be a cyclic extension of number fields. Then \(\alpha \in K^\times\) is in the norm image of \(L^\times\) if and only if \(\alpha\) is in the local norm image \(\mathrm{Norm}_{L_{\mathfrak{q}}/K_{\mathfrak{p}}}(L_{\mathfrak{q}}^\times)\) for all primes \(\mathfrak{q}\) of \(L\).

This theorem is a consequence of global class field theory and is an important ingredient in the proof of the Hasse-Minkowski theorem. However, the Hasse Norm Theorem is true only for cyclic extensions: it fails even for the simplest class of non-cyclic abelian extensions – biquadratic extensions. We have already all but constructed a class of counterexamples:

  1. Take a prime \(p \equiv 1 \bmod 4\) and choose another prime \(q \equiv 1 \bmod 4\) such that \(q \equiv \square \bmod p\). Set \(K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p}, \sqrt{q})\).
  2. Per our earlier discussion, \(p\) and \(q\) are the only ramified primes in \(K\) and both split in \(K\) as well. Moreover every local extension has degree \(1\) or \(2\), so all squares are local norms everywhere.
  3. Let \(S_p\) be the set of rational primes which split completely in \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p})\); for \(x \in \mathbb{Q}^\times\) we define the function \[\varphi(x) = \prod_{l \in S_p} (q,x)_l\] where \((a,b)_l\) is the Hilbert symbol. It turns out3 that \(\ker \varphi = \set{x \in \mathbb{Q}^\times : x^2 \in \mathrm{Norm}_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(K^\times)}\), and when \(x\) is divisible only by odd primes \(l\) such that \(\left(\frac{l}{p}\right) = -1\), we have \(\varphi(x) = \left(\frac{x}{q}\right)\).
  4. Thus if (for example) \(l\) is any prime which is inert in both \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{p})\) and \(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{q})\), then \(l^2\) will be a local norm everywhere but not a global norm.
